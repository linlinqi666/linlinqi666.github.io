iGEM SZPU 2026

Yeast-Based Influenza Biosensor

Background

Influenza A virus (H1N1) spreads primarily through airborne droplets and aerosols, remaining a persistent global health threat.

WHO FLUNET data (June 2021 – March 2026) reveals dramatic fluctuations:

  • During COVID-19 (2021-2022): NPIs (masking, distancing, travel restrictions) suppressed influenza to historic lows.
  • Post-pandemic rebound (2023-2026): As NPIs lifted, influenza resurged — positivity rates hit nearly 50% in some regions, with rapid subtype shifts from H3N2 to B(Victoria).

The critical gap: Current surveillance relies on symptomatic patients seeking clinical testing. But the virus spreads through the air before symptoms appear. Conventional methods (PCR) require specialized labs and cause 1-2 weeks of reporting delay.

What's missing: A tool that detects influenza virus directly in the air — rapid, affordable, and point-of-use.

That is what EchoYeast delivers.

Inspiration

FLUNET data reveals a pattern: after COVID-19 restrictions lifted, H3N2 surged while H1N1 remained stable. But H3N2 mutates too rapidly — a sensor for it would quickly become obsolete.

We initially tried building an H3N2 sensor. The limitations were clear: high mutation rate makes long-term detection unreliable.

So we made a strategic choice: start with H1N1 — slower evolution, more stable target, safer proof of concept.

The innovation: Once validated, we can swap the nanobody to detect other subtypes (including new H3N2 variants). No redesign. No starting from zero.

Thus, EchoYeast was born: a yeast biosensor that turns virus recognition into an instant visible signal (GFP or color change).

Our vision: A sustainable, reprogrammable platform. When the virus evolves, we just swap the recognition module — not the whole system.
