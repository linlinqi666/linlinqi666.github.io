Our Human Practices

For whom does our science aim to solve problems?

What is it?

HP is our dialogue with the world HP is not an "additional question" for the project, but a feedback adjustment mechanism that runs through the entire process. At iGEM, HP stands for two things: 1. How does the world affect our work 2. How does our work impact the world Let science step out of the laboratory, into the crowd, and then return with the voices of the crowd.

Why?

Because there are real pain points in flu testing We develop the biosensor for influenza A virus not for showing off our skills, but because we see the cracks in reality: 1. Traditional detection is too slow (PCR takes too long) 2. Professional equipment is too expensive (grassroots hospitals cannot afford it) 3. The operation is too complicated (ordinary people can't use it) 4. The pressure of screening during the flu season is high, and the risk of cross-infection is high If new technologies are only evaluated based on whether they can be developed, without considering whether they should be developed and whether they will be accepted, even the most scientific advancements may become useless or even harmful. So we do HP to make the yeast sensor truly become a beacon guarding the breath of all mankind.

How to do it?

How to do it? -- Go out, listen, reflect, and change Step 1: Listen to authentic sounds 1. Seek advice from doctors and experts (in synthetic biology, virology, and public policy) 2. Entering the community (questionnaire feedback) 3. Collaborate with other iGEM teams
Step 2: Enable sound to change the experiment ("listening-reflecting-changing" closed loop) External voices, our changes Expert/Doctor: Enhance GFP reporting system by relying on detection equipment, aiming for visual signal interpretation Community feedback: Is it safe? Research on the biosafety of yeast
Step 3: Our contribution 1. A cheaper, faster, and more user-friendly testing solution for influenza A 2. Cases of the integration of synthetic biology and social needs 3. A group of young scientists who are willing to listen and dare to make changes

We believe

Good science is not just a cold pile of data. It should listen, breathe, and change. It should respond to an old man's worries, a doctor's fatigue, and a child's curiosity. This is our Human Practices. It's not a task, but the reason why we do science.

02

DBTL Integration

Iterative design informed by surveillance data and public feedback

Design

Based on WHO FluNet and China CDC surveillance data (2021-2026), we identified that COVID-19 containment measures suppressed influenza A transmission to historically low levels, while the post-pandemic era has seen significant rebound with record-high positivity rates and rapid viral mutations. Current detection methods rely on clinical sampling, suffer from reporting delays, and cannot monitor airborne viruses.

HP Insight

"Existing detection methods present clear application limitations. We need a solution that can monitor airborne viruses in real-time." — Surveillance Data Analysis

Build

We determined our project direction: abandoning the highly mutable H3N2 subtype in favour of developing a reprogrammable yeast biosensor targeting the more stable H1N1 subtype. This design allows adaptation to new strains simply by replacing the recognition module. Concurrently, we conducted a public questionnaire survey to assess public awareness of influenza A, demand for household airborne influenza detection tools, and preferences regarding detection speed, result display methods, operational procedures, pricing, and usage scenarios.

HP Insight

"Users prefer rapid, low-cost, and user-friendly household detection solutions." — Public Survey Results
